Many people express surprise when they learn that I went through the arduous and expensive investment of obtaining an M.D. only to decide against working directly with patients. As a medical editor, I have been able to leverage my education, but I have not yet fully honored the personal sacrifice it took to graduate from Duke Med by having the kind of impact on society that I intended.

The world’s best medical research teams and med tech/biotech companies will increasingly require experts who can seamlessly navigate the worlds of software development and clinical science, eliminating the need for “translation” between medical researchers and the engineers who write the code. I want to be that missing link, and being awarded an Impact Scholarship will help remove a huge financial barrier that would otherwise hinder my developing what I believe will be a valued and sought after skill-set intersecting software and medicine.

Every single human disease, from Alzheimer’s to cancer to malaria, occurs at the molecular level within cells. Because the molecular pathways of most diseases are incredibly complex, identifying the aberrant processes and effectively intervening to prevent or treat disease is inherently difficult. Additionally, the need for therapies that are specifically tailored to individual patients is becoming increasingly crucial because of the subtle genetic and molecular nuances between individuals. Instead of continuing to be peripherally involved in med tech and biotech advances aimed at addressing these issues (eg, through learning about the startups in the Google Ventures Life Sciences portfolio, working on marketing campaigns for novel urine-based cancer diagnostics, or coordinating the Healthcare/Life Sciences track in the Duke Startup Challenge Business Plan competition), the programming expertise I will begin developing at Coding Dojo will allow me to more directly work on some of the most important advances in healthcare. This is what I was meant to do.

Watching helplessly as my grandmother was taken from us too soon by the ravaging effects of Alzheimer’s, I was motivated to pursue medicine. Now, as my mom is beginning to exhibit early signs of this unforgiving disease, I think about the lack of any truly effective treatment, despite decades of biomedical investigation and billions of research dollars. Turning the tide against this disease and accelerating the pace of discoveries will require the development of bioinformatics methodologies; these include software tools and computational approaches that will lead to treatments that actually target the toxic proteins and restore cognitive function. I intend to develop the skills necessary to effectively contribute to these efforts in the hope that diseases like Alzheimer’s can be cured or at least effectively treated through software-based advances.

Completing the Coding Dojo bootcamp will have a significant personal impact on me, by allowing me to work on software solutions that will enhance our understanding of complex diseases and lead to effective treatments. In studying medicine, I particularly enjoyed determining the specific location of a brain lesion based on neurological symptoms, an exercise that most med students hated. Similarly, as I have begun learning how software programs work, I find myself completely engrossed in coding challenges. I enjoy a true sense of accomplishment from searching through lines of code to find the specific syntax or logic error that's preventing my code from running; this is likely a reflection of the unique personality traits that also lent themselves to my medical editing career, where precision and accuracy are paramount. Most important, however, is the fact that my future career as a software developer in healthcare has the potential to positively impact the health and well being of millions of patients. That is an exciting prospect.




About Me

Born and raised in Trinidad & Tobago

I spent the first 18 years of my life in my native T&T. I attended Presentation College in San Fernando (Pres), one of the most respected highschools in the country, where I enjoyed both academic and extra-curricular success. While consistently performing in the top 5% of students academically for most of my highschool career, I also represented my highschool/the T&T National Team in several sports (football, cricket, squash, etc) and developed into a leader. I was captain of the National Jr Squash team and was nominated in '98 for the WITCO National Sportsman of the Year Award. Based on my track record of academic excellence, demonstrated leadership, and all-round success, I was awarded a full academic scholarship to attend college in the US.

During these years, I developed a deep fascination with understanding how complex systems work. Because it is one of the most complex systems, I gravitated towards the human body with aspirations to study medicine and neuroscience. Coming of age during the rise of the dot coms in the '90's also inspired an interest in technology, particularly bio-tech and med-tech.


Moved to Atlanta to attend Morehouse College as a pre-medical student

At Morehouse, I studied Biology with a concentration in Neuroscience and won research fellowships and research assistantships at Duke University, University of Pittsburgh, Emory University, Morehouse School of Medicine and University of Manchester, UK. As part of the Honors Program with an intense schedule of extrememly demanding classes as well as volunteer and other extra-curricular activities, I maintained a GPA above 3.75 for most of my undergraduate career.

Based on my academic and overall performance as a pre-med student I was accepted to several top-tier medical schools, including Yale, Baylor, and Duke, and I ultimately matriculated at Duke University School of Medicine


Graduated with a Doctor of Medicine (M.D.) degree from Duke University School of Medicine

After undergrad, I attended Duke for medical school. There, I earned high-pass or honors in almost every clinical course and won a $25K 3rd Year Thesis Scholarship for research on free-radical brain injury after stroke. I also served as Co-President of the Duke Chapter of the SNMA (Student National Medical Association) and led the organization to a Student Organization of the Year Award.

I served as the Healthcare/Life Sciences Track lead of the Duke StartUp Challenge Elevator Pitch/Business Plan competition and was subsequently a Judge as well. These experiences contnued to fuel my interest in entrepreneurship and technology.


After Medical School

After Duke, I worked in both clinical research and basic science research related to brain injury before pursuing a career as a bio-medical/clinical editor in academic publishing and then healthcare advertising.

From early 2015, I started teaching myself programming using various online resources and books. Because I enjoyed these educational forays more than my professional job, I began considering options for a career change.


Software Engineering

I identified Coding Dojo as my software engineering boot-camp of choice because it was one of a handful that taught Python (relevant for hard science applications and machine learning) and because the 1000+ hr curriculum would have allowed me to become a polyglot programmer while learning the basics of computer science fundamentals in a matter of months. I applied for and won their Impact Scholarship Grand Prize, which covered 90% of the tuition. My scholarship essay (see Why Software) describes my deep motivations for my career change and outlines the general vision for my career.

In the months following graduation from the coding bootcamp, I continued deepening my coding skills and was selected as one of 22 participants (from almost 400 applications nationwide) in the Microsoft LEAP Software Engineering Apprenticeship Program. The program consists of a classroom-based portion that covers computer science and programming fundamentals as well as an apprenticeship with a Microsoft product team (I am part of the Microsoft Bing iOS Team)


Future Career

My immediate goal following the LEAP program is to work as a Software Engineer in a role that provides ample opportunities for growth as an engineer while writing code for products that improve lives.
